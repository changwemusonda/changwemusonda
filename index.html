Experience

Engineering is about more than technical skill. It is about showing up consistently, adapting under pressure, and making the people around you better. Every role here has shaped how I approach that.

Autonomy Lead

Team Pegasus — Lockheed Martin Sponsored Research & Senior Capstone
January 2025 – May 2026

I joined Team Pegasus in January 2025 as an Autonomy Engineer on a Lockheed Martin-sponsored research project building an end-to-end autonomy stack for a 110 lb eVTOL UAV. As the project matured and I took on the Autonomy Lead role, I chose to carry it forward as my senior capstone. I own the full autonomy pipeline (perception, path planning, and control), built in ROS 2 and PX4 on Jetson-based ARM compute, integrating LiDAR, stereo cameras, and IMUs for real-time state estimation and navigation. Leading a seven-person team across autonomy, avionics, and hardware has meant translating technical constraints into decisions the whole team can act on. When hardware-software integration failures surfaced during flight testing, I dug into telemetry logs, found the root causes, and resolved them.

Research Assistant

Autonomous Vehicle Laboratory, California State Polytechnic University, Pomona
May 2025 – Present

I work in the Autonomous Vehicle Laboratory on campus developing real-time robotic software in C++ and Python for autonomous ground vehicle systems. My work centers on multi-sensor fusion, integrating LiDAR, stereo cameras, and Xsens IMUs into ROS 2 pipelines for real-time localization and mapping. A significant part of the role is iterative field testing: running the system, analyzing where it breaks down, and refining the approach. I have also taken ownership of system documentation and calibration procedures so the whole team can reproduce results and build on each other's work.

Academic

Deep Differentiable Neural Planner

An extension of a CVPR 2022 paper on differentiable planning for egocentric navigation. I benchmarked three planning architectures (VIN, GPPN, and CALVIN variants) to evaluate their performance and scalability in learned planning settings. The work goes beyond evaluation: once I identified where each architecture breaks down, the goal became understanding how those weaknesses can be addressed and what improvements would move differentiable planning closer to real-world navigation.

A* Path Planning on FPGA

A hardware-accelerated implementation of the A* path planning algorithm in Verilog on an FPGA, with a Python interface for validation. I designed the hardware logic for parallel execution and validated performance against Python reference models across multiple test scenarios. The project required reasoning about timing constraints, resource utilization, and the gap between algorithmic logic and hardware reality.
